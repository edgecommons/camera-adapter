How fast the adapter clears a line of cameras — and what actually gates it

A synchronized burst (every camera captures at once — the line-clearance pattern) at 75, 100 and 125 warm ONVIF-RTSP cameras, with the adapter alone on an NVMe SSD box and the camera fleet on another machine across the wire. Every camera captured; nothing was dropped, disconnected, or restarted at any scale.

Clearance time — trigger to last image durably on disk

The whole line clears in a handful of seconds, and it scales gently: adding 50 cameras (75→125) adds ~1.7 s, not proportionally more. This is the warm case — sessions already connected, the realistic line-clearance state.

Throughput is flat — the concurrency cap is not the limit

Throughput sits at ~22–25 captures/second regardless of how many cameras. The last bar (125*) is the same 125-camera burst with the in-flight concurrency cap raised from 32 to 125 — and it barely moved (25→27/s). If the 32-wide capture gate were the bottleneck, opening it 4× would have opened the throughput. It didn't. Something downstream is the real ceiling.

Where each capture's time goes — the catalog dominates

With the concurrency cap wide open (so admission queueing is removed), the median capture still spends ~1 second in queue wait — which here is not admission but writing the capture's ACCEPTED and QUEUED rows — and the persist stage carries the terminal catalog write. Both are the durable SQLite catalog (a 2-worker pool, WAL, synchronous=FULL, ~3 fsync'd transactions per capture). The middle stage, acquire, is the RTSP keyframe wait plus H.264 decode — a property of the camera stream, not the adapter.

Gating factor: the durable catalog write. A synchronized burst serializes through the 2-worker SQLite pool, one fsync barrier per state transition. Raising the capture-concurrency cap doesn't help because the captures still funnel through those two workers. This is the same mechanism finding B6 named and the edge-storage (X5) run confirmed — here it is the throughput ceiling on fast storage too, just at a much higher rate. Every capture is durably committed with a sha256 and a metadata sidecar, which is exactly what a 21 CFR Part 11 line-clearance record requires; the durability is the cost, and it is the cost that gates.

Per-capture latency — median vs the tail

Half the cameras have their image within ~1.8–2.4 s of the trigger; the slowest (the last through the catalog) lands by ~3.2–4.6 s. The tail widens with N because more captures queue behind the same two catalog workers.

Resource scaling — linear and modest

Memory and threads grow linearly with camera count: roughly 26 MB and ~16 threads per warm RTSP session (each holds a GStreamer pipeline). At 125 cameras the adapter used ~3.3 GB and ~2,000 threads — comfortable on the 64 GB / 32-core box. Nothing here is the bottleneck.

What this means for a 75-camera line clearance

~3 seconds to capture and durably store all 75 images, with zero loss, on an SSD adapter box with pre-connected cameras. 100 cameras ≈ 4.5 s, 125 ≈ 5 s. The line does not need to stagger for throughput — every camera captures in the same synchronized burst, and the adapter admits and clears them all.

If you need it faster than ~25 captures/second, the lever is not more concurrency — it is the durable-write path: more catalog workers (the default is 2) or splitting the durable store. And if clearances are more than ~30 s apart, warm RTSP sessions idle out and re-establish (the first burst above ran a touch slower, ~3.4 vs 3.2 s, for that reason); keeping sessions warm, or using an ONVIF snapshot / GenICam trigger, avoids that.

Honesty about the method

This is a real cross-host measurement: the adapter ran alone on lab-5950x (AMD 5950X, NVMe SSD — real disk, not tmpfs) and the fleet on a separate machine, traffic over the LAN. Caveats worth stating plainly:

  • Cameras were warm ONVIF-RTSP streams at 1280×720; the ~0.5 s acquire is the stream's 1-second GOP. A machine-vision camera (GenICam trigger, or a shorter GOP) would acquire faster; the catalog ceiling would be unchanged.
  • The fleet served one mediamtx stream fanned out to N readers — adapter-side concurrency is genuine, but source diversity is not. A first pass silently capped at 64 cameras: that was the test simulator's connection ceiling, not the adapter (found and made configurable).
  • Storage was NVMe. On edge-class eMMC/SD the same burst is ~8× slower (measured separately) — the catalog gate scales with fsync latency.
  • These are single-burst measurements at each N, not a sustained multi-hour soak.
